Scenario Description

A faculty member teaching a Qualitative Research Methods course wants to demonstrate how artificial intelligence (AI) can serve as a supportive assistant during coding and theme identification, while maintaining human interpretation and methodological rigor.

The instructor designs a short module called “AI as an Assistant in Thematic Analysis.” The students such as graduate or upper-level undergraduates analyze short interview transcripts about learners’ online study experiences. In the first class, they practice manual open coding; in the next, they use AI tools such as ChatGPT, MAXQDA, NVivo, and Gemini to generate, compare, and refine codes and themes.

Through this process, students explore how AI can enhance efficiency and reveal patterns, while human researchers evaluate, merge, or reject AI outputs based on context and theory. The instructor also leads reflection on trust, authorship, bias, and data privacy to ensure ethical and transparent use of AI.

Learning Objectives

  • Identify how AI tools can support qualitative data analysis, particularly in coding and theme identification.
  • Apply a human–AI collaborative process using systematic thematic analysis steps.
  • Evaluate the transparency, trustworthiness, and interpretive ownership of AI-assisted outputs.
  • Reflect on ethical and methodological considerations of using AI in qualitative research.

Teaching Activities and Process

Session 1 – Manual Coding Foundations (90 minutes)

Goal: Understand traditional qualitative coding and prepare for AI integration.
  • Mini-lecture (15 min): Instructor reviews the six steps of systematic thematic analysis (Naeem et al., 2025) and explains different levels of AI involvement: human-only, human-initiated, AI-initiated (Puranik et al., 2025). Emphasize that AI is an assistant, not a supervisor.
  • Demonstration (20 min): Instructor manually codes a short transcript, modeling how to label meaningful segments, group codes, and link them to research questions.
  • Pair Practice (40 min): Students manually code a second excerpt in small groups using Sheets or NVivo. Each group lists codes, supporting quotes, and short notes on interpretation.
  • Class Debrief (15 min): What challenges arose in manual coding? How did interpretive choices differ between peers? Where might AI help streamline or challenge your process?

Session 2 – AI-Assisted Coding (90 minutes)

Goal: Experience AI-assisted coding, compare with manual results, and reflect on interpretive ownership.
  • Prompt Design (10 min): Instructor introduces clear prompt structures for AI coding, e.g., “You are assisting a qualitative researcher conducting thematic analysis on interviews about online learning. Using the 6 Rs framework (Realness, Richness, Repetition, Rationale, Repartee, Regal), generate initial codes and short explanations.”
  • AI Exploration (30 min): Students use ChatGPT or Gemini with prepared transcripts; each group records prompts and AI-generated codes with meanings.
  • Comparison & Merging (30 min): Students compare AI vs. human codes; label (AI/Human); color-code overlaps; merge refine or reject codes with short justifications (e.g., “AI missed emotional nuance in participant quote”).
  • Reflection Discussion (20 min): When did AI broaden your perspective? When did it misinterpret the context? How did you maintain ownership of interpretation?

Session 3 – Theme Development & Reflection (Optional, 60–90 min)

Goal: Move from codes to themes and demonstrate reflexivity and conceptual reasoning.
  • Theme Development (30 min): Groups cluster related codes into themes guided by the 4 Rs: Reciprocal, Recognizable, Responsive, Resourceful (Naeem et al., 2025).
  • Visualization (20 min): Groups create a theme map (NVivo/MAXQDA) showing the relationship between AI and human insights.
  • Individual Reflection (20–30 min): Each student writes a 300–400-word memo about efficiency, bias, and interpretive depth in AI-assisted coding.

Student Artifacts

Page requirement: 2–3 pages.

  • Manual Coding Table: Human-generated codes and supporting excerpts.
  • AI Coding Table: AI-generated codes with sample prompts.
  • Comparison & Merged Themes Map: Integrated analysis with color-coding and short notes.
  • Reflective Memo: Discussion of authorship, bias, efficiency, and learning outcomes.

Guidelines for Faculty

Preparation

  • Select short, anonymized transcripts (at least 3 pages each).
  • Prepare clear, structured prompts for AI coding (see Naeem et al., 2025 frameworks).
  • Test AI tools in advance to ensure safe and private use.

During Teaching

  • Encourage students to think of AI as a thinking partner — not an evaluator.
  • Model transparency: label outputs as AI-generated, human-coded, or merged.
  • Highlight moments showing AI “breadth” vs. human “depth.”

After Teaching

  • Use the rubric to assess critical reasoning rather than AI accuracy.
  • Facilitate a debrief discussion about ethics, ownership, and teaching implications.

Guidelines for Students

  1. Manual coding first. Identify meaningful ideas and patterns manually in the transcript before using AI.
  2. Use AI to propose codes. In ChatGPT or Gemini, include the research question, purpose, and brief context.
  3. Compare and merge. Align AI and human codes; label as AI, Human, or Merged; color-code overlaps; write brief justifications.
  4. Create a thematic map (using the 4 Rs framework).
    • Select related codes and cluster them into higher-level themes and subthemes.
    • Show clear relationships or overlaps between codes (for example, through arrows or proximity on the map).
    • Label whether insights originate from AI, human analysis, or both.
    • Ensure your final map clearly shows how codes developed into themes that answer the research question.
  5. Write a short reflective memo. How AI influenced your analytic thinking and pattern recognition? Which parts of the process (coding, theming, mapping) required human judgment most? How you ensured interpretive ownership and data transparency? What ethical or methodological lessons you will apply in future research?
